# Video Conventions for Short-Form Content

Read this before writing any video script. These conventions govern pacing, structure, and delivery for on-camera teleprompter scripts — primarily 1-minute shorts/reels.

---

## Format: On-Camera Teleprompter

The speaker reads from a teleprompter on camera. B-roll overlays are handled by an editor. The script is the teleprompter text with b-roll suggestions as margin notes.

- No timing marks — actual read time determines pacing
- No stage directions beyond b-roll suggestions
- No section headers in the script body — continuous spoken delivery
- B-roll notes are right-aligned suggestions for the editor, not instructions for the speaker

---

## Word Budget

| Format | Words | Duration |
|--------|-------|----------|
| Short (default) | 150-180 | ~60 seconds |
| Extended short | 200-250 | ~90 seconds |
| Long short | 300-350 | ~2 minutes |

Default to the short format. Go longer only with user approval.

---

## Hook Structure

The hook is the first 5-10 words. It must create a question or tension that the viewer needs resolved.

**Hook patterns that work:**

| Pattern | Example | Why it works |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Counterintuitive finding | "The most engaged learners performed the worst." | Creates cognitive dissonance |
| Specific number | "72% of leaders said yes — and were wrong." | Specificity signals real data |
| Direct challenge | "You've been thinking about retention backwards." | Stakes the viewer's assumption |
| Incomplete story | "We built the most-watched video on their channel. Then deleted the approach." | Opens a narrative gap |

**Hook patterns that fail:**

| Pattern | Example | Why it fails |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Broad question | "Have you ever wondered about learning?" | No tension, easy to scroll past |
| Definition opening | "Engagement is defined as..." | Teaching mode before earning attention |
| "In this video" | "In this video, I'll explain..." | Meta-commentary, not content |
| Vague promise | "This will change how you think about everything." | Unearned claim, no specificity |

## Pacing: The Beat Structure

Each paragraph in the script is a **beat** — a complete thought with a natural pause after it. A 1-minute script has 4-6 beats.

**Beat sequence for a 1-minute script:**

| Beat | Function | Words |
|------|----------|-------|
| 1 | **Hook** — create the question | 15-25 |
| 2 | **Ground** — context the viewer needs | 25-35 |
| 3 | **Turn** — the finding, connection, or surprise | 35-50 |
| 4 | **Land** — what this means for the viewer | 25-35 |
| 5 | **CTA** — connect to the article | 20-30 |

Not every script follows this exact sequence. The principle is: **hook, build, turn, land.** The turn is the moment the viewer's assumption shifts — it should arrive in the middle third, not at the end.

## Spoken Language Conventions

**Write for the ear:**
- Short sentences. Subject-verb-object.
- One idea per sentence. One sentence per thought.
- Contractions always ("don't" not "do not", "it's" not "it is")
- Active voice. "The research found" not "It was found by the research"
- Concrete nouns over abstract ones. "Leaders" not "stakeholders." "The study" not "the body of evidence."

**What doesn't survive spoken delivery:**
- Semicolons, em dashes, parentheticals
- Subordinate clauses longer than 5 words
- Lists of more than 3 items
- Academic hedging ("it could be argued that," "there is some evidence to suggest")
- Transitions that work in prose but not speech ("furthermore," "moreover," "consequently")

**What works on camera:**
- "Here's the thing." (reorientation)
- "And that's exactly what happened." (confirmation before the turn)
- "But." (beat pause, then the counterpoint)
- Direct address: "you" not "one" or "people"
- Rhetorical questions that the next beat answers

## B-Roll Suggestions

B-roll notes are suggestions for the editor, not requirements. They describe what would reinforce the spoken content visually.

**Format:** Right-aligned, bracketed, on their own line between beats:

```
The research tracked 200 classrooms over two years.

                                        [B-ROLL: classroom footage or data visualization]

What they found surprised everyone.
```

**Good b-roll suggestions:**
- Describe the visual concept, not the specific footage
- One suggestion per beat transition (not every sentence)
- Suggest what supports the spoken content, not what decorates it

**Skip b-roll suggestions when:**
- The speaker's delivery IS the content (emotional beats, direct challenges)
- The beat is too short for a visual transition

## CTA Conventions

The call-to-action connects the video to the article. It must be specific about what the viewer will find — not a generic pointer.

**Good CTAs:**
- "The full article breaks down all seven patterns — and the one that reverses when you scale. Link in the description."
- "If you want the actual numbers behind this, the article has the complete study. Link below."
- "We go deep on the constraint that made this work in the full piece. Link in the description."

**Bad CTAs:**
- "Check out the full article for more." (more what?)
- "Link in the description." (alone, with no reason to click)
- "Read the article to learn more about this topic." (generic)

The CTA should make the viewer feel like the video gave them something complete AND there's something different waiting in the article — not that the video was a teaser.

## Voice Adaptation for Video

The video uses the same voice profile as the article but in a different register. The person on camera is the same person who wrote the article — but talking, not writing.

**What shifts:**
- Sentence length shortens
- Register becomes more direct
- Teaching/explaining mode leads over analytical mode
- Personal authority surfaces more ("In my experience" is natural on camera, less so in prose)

**What stays the same:**
- Domain stance (insider, not narrator)
- Thinking patterns (how they reason through problems)
- Relationship to the subject matter
- Epistemic standards (same sourcing discipline)
